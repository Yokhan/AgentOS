# Russian Explanation And Persuasion

This profile is a child of `writing.md` and `russian-writing-profile.md`. Load it
for Russian informational, marketing, educational, presentation, and substantial
communication text that must explain a difficult idea or support a decision.

The method is derived from public, attributable material for Maxim Ilyakhov's
`Ясно, понятно`, the open `Информационный стиль` textbook, `Как написать`, and
the registered project sources. The synthesis and examples are template-authored,
not copied book content.

## Explanation Contract

Determine:

1. What the reader currently believes, knows, fears, or assumes.
2. What should change after the text and which decision or action follows.
3. Which context can block correct interpretation before the argument begins.
4. Which terms, causal links, examples, and evidence the reader needs.
5. Which objections are reasonable and which claims remain uncertain.
6. What must be understandable from headings, captions, tables, or first screen
   even if the reader does not read linearly.

## Operational Rules

### 1. Prepare Context Before Arguing

Do not begin with proof when the reader interprets the topic through a different
frame. Surface the relevant situation, stakes, definitions, and shared facts
first. Never manipulate context by hiding material alternatives or limits.

### 2. Move From Known To New

Introduce one meaningful dependency at a time. Define an unfamiliar concept
before using it to explain another unfamiliar concept. Experts may keep precise
terms; explain them according to the reader's preparation instead of replacing
them with inaccurate everyday words.

### 3. Build Interest From Stakes And Change

Interest comes from a relevant question, conflict, consequence, discovery, or
choice. It does not require clickbait, artificial suspense, or decorative drama.
Show why the idea matters in the reader's world before adding detail.

### 4. Use Examples To Expose The Rule

After an abstract claim, show a concrete case and identify what it demonstrates.
Use an anti-example to reveal a boundary or common error. Examples make a
mechanism visible; they do not prove frequency, safety, or universal causality.

### 5. Use Analogies With An Exit

State what the analogy maps and where it stops mapping. Prefer a direct mechanism
when the analogy would create a false model. Never substitute metaphor for a
technical or legal definition.

### 6. Show Causality, Not Sequence Alone

Distinguish `after`, `because`, `correlates with`, and `may contribute to`.
Identify actors, conditions, intermediate steps, and counterfactuals when they
matter. Calibrate certainty to evidence.

### 7. Address The Strong Objection

Represent the reader's reasonable objection accurately, then answer it with
evidence, boundary, trade-off, or an explicit unknown. Do not invent a weak
opponent to make the preferred conclusion look inevitable.

### 8. Make Structure Visible Without Reading

Headings, captions, tables, diagrams, and highlighted conclusions should carry a
coherent overview. Visual treatment must clarify relationships, scale, sequence,
or comparison; decoration is not explanation. Text alternatives remain required.

### 9. Separate Explanation From Persuasion

First make the mechanism and alternatives understandable. Then state the
recommended decision and why it fits the declared goal. Persuasion may organize
truth; it may not fabricate evidence, suppress material limits, or exploit fear.

### 10. End With The New Model Or Action

Restate what changed in the reader's understanding, the applicable boundary, and
the next decision or action. A summary should compress the causal model, not
repeat the introduction with synonyms.

## Original Examples

### Missing Context

**Weak:** `Новая система расчёта справедливее старой.`

**Diagnosis:** the reader does not know the old failure, affected group, or
meaning of fairness.

**Stronger:** `Старая система считала только число закрытых заявок, поэтому
сложные обращения снижали рейтинг специалиста. Новая учитывает время, сложность и
оценку клиента. Специалист больше не теряет рейтинг за обращение, которое требует
нескольких дней.`

### Unknown Through Unknown

**Weak:** `Шардирование уменьшает contention благодаря распределённому consensus.`

**Diagnosis:** two unexplained concepts are used to explain each other.

**Stronger:** `Шардирование делит данные между несколькими узлами. Запросы к
разным частям выполняются параллельно и реже конкурируют за один ресурс. Но
операции между частями усложняются: узлам приходится согласовывать общий результат.`

### Example Without A Rule

**Weak:** `Например, команда А выросла на 20%. Значит, метод работает.`

**Diagnosis:** one case is treated as general proof and the mechanism is absent.

**Stronger:** `В команде А время ответа сократилось после общей очереди заявок.
Этот пример показывает предполагаемый механизм, но не доказывает, что рост вызван
только очередью: одновременно команда наняла двух специалистов.`

### Analogy Without A Boundary

**Weak:** `Нейросеть думает как человек.`

**Diagnosis:** the analogy attributes unsupported human mechanisms.

**Stronger:** `Нейросеть выбирает продолжение по закономерностям обучающих данных.
Это похоже на автодополнение по масштабу вариантов, но не означает человеческого
понимания, намерения или опыта.`

### Hidden Objection

**Weak:** `Перейти на новый тариф — очевидно выгодное решение.`

**Diagnosis:** cost and switching risk are omitted, so the conclusion is not
decision-ready.

**Stronger:** `Новый тариф дешевле после 40 активных пользователей. До этого
порога старый стоит меньше. Переход потребует заново настроить роли; рекомендуем
его командам от 40 человек, которые готовы выделить администратора на один день.`

### Decorative Visual

**Weak:** a slide repeats the paragraph beside an unrelated stock illustration.

**Diagnosis:** the visual consumes attention but shows no relationship.

**Stronger:** replace it with a three-step diagram showing input, transformation,
and verified result; keep the detailed caveat in the speaker notes and provide a
text equivalent.

## Acceptance Gate

- Is the reader's starting model explicit enough to choose the right context?
- Does each unfamiliar concept depend only on already established concepts?
- Are stakes and interest truthful rather than manufactured?
- Do examples reveal a mechanism without pretending to be universal proof?
- Are analogy boundaries and causal certainty explicit?
- Is the strongest reasonable objection answered fairly?
- Can headings and visuals communicate the core model without linear reading?
- Are explanation, recommendation, and requested action distinguishable?
